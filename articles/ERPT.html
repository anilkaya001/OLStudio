    Estimating Exchange Rate Pass-Through (ERPT)

    Contents

    Abstract

    Exchange rate pass-through (ERPT) is practically horizon-indexed mapping from an exchange-rate disturbance to domestic-currency prices. Empirical estimates of ERPT differ across methods because the underlying estimands differ: some specifications measure conditional responses to observed exchange-rate changes, while IRF-based approaches measure responses to an identified exchange-rate shock within a dynamic system. This note formalizes these estimands and emphasizes sources of error that arise when economic structure is not aligned with model configuration, with particular focus on small open economy settings.

    Estimand and sign conventions

    Let $p_t$ denote the log of a domestic-currency price index, and let $e_t$ denote the log nominal exchange rate expressed as domestic currency per unit of foreign currency. Under this convention, a depreciation corresponds to an increase in $e_t$ and therefore $\Delta e_t > 0$. Let $\mathcal{F}_{t-1}$ denote an information set, and let $\varepsilon^e_t$ denote the structural exchange-rate disturbance under a maintained identification scheme.

    A structural horizon-$h$ pass-through estimand is $$\text{ERPT}(h)\equiv \frac{\partial (p_{t+h}-p_{t-1})}{\partial \varepsilon^e_t},\quad h\ge 0.$$ This definition is explicit about (i) the price object, (ii) the horizon, and (iii) the shock. Any empirical estimate that does not state all three is not directly comparable across studies.

    When the regressor is $\Delta e_t$ rather than $\varepsilon^e_t$, the estimated coefficient generally corresponds to a response to a mixture of shocks that move the exchange rate, with mixture weights determined by the sample variance decomposition of $\Delta e_t$. IRF-based models attempt to isolate a single shock, while non-IRF specifications typically require an additional identification device to interpret coefficients structurally.

    Method classes and what they measure

    A useful classification separates models by whether ERPT is extracted from an impulse response function (IRF) to an identified shock or from a conditional mean regression on observed exchange-rate changes. In the import-price regression tradition, distributed-lag specifications estimate the mapping from $\Delta e_t$ to $\Delta p_t$ and cumulate coefficients to obtain a horizon-$h$ response, as in widely used empirical approaches (e.g., Campa & Goldberg, 2005). In system-based approaches, ERPT is defined as a (cumulated) IRF of $p$ to an exchange-rate shock identified inside a VAR/SVAR, or estimated horizon-by-horizon via local projections (e.g., Jordà, 2005).

    Non-IRF models: distributed lags and ECM

    Distributed-lag specification

    A baseline reduced-form specification is $$\Delta p_t=\alpha+\sum_{k=0}^{K}\beta_k\Delta e_{t-k}+\delta' w_t+u_t,$$ where $w_t$ includes observed covariates (foreign costs, commodity prices, demand proxies, policy instruments, seasonal components, and time effects). A commonly reported horizon-$h$ response is the cumulative multiplier $$B(h)=\sum_{k=0}^{h}\beta_k.$$

    Interpreting $B(h)$ as a structural pass-through requires a conditional mean independence restriction such as $E[u_t\mid \Delta e_t,\Delta e_{t-1},\ldots,w_t,\mathcal{F}_{t-1}]=0$. This is a strong condition in macroeconomic data: exchange-rate movements often respond to shocks that also shift marginal costs, inflation expectations, and policy rates contemporaneously. A key implication is that high-frequency exchange-rate volatility can make $B(0)$ sensitive to measurement conventions, while medium-horizon $B(h)$ is sensitive to omitted persistent drivers.

    ECM and long-run pass-through

    When $p_t$ and $e_t$ are persistent, an error-correction representation separates long-run equilibrium sensitivity from short-run adjustment. A long-run relation may be expressed as $$p_t=\phi_0+\phi_1 e_t+\phi_2 c_t+\varepsilon_t,$$ where $c_t$ is a log foreign-cost proxy, and cointegration corresponds to stationary $\varepsilon_t$.

    The associated ECM is $$\Delta p_t=\alpha+\lambda\varepsilon_{t-1}+\sum_{k=0}^{K}\beta_k\Delta e_{t-k}+\sum_{k=0}^{K}\gamma_k\Delta c_{t-k}+\eta_t.$$ Under stability of the cointegrating relation, $\phi_1$ is interpreted as long-run ERPT in levels, and $\lambda$ governs the speed of reversion to the long-run manifold. If the long-run relation is unstable due to regime changes, trade composition shifts, or persistent policy breaks, $\phi_1$ becomes sample-specific and can conflate different structural regimes.

    IRF-based ERPT: VAR/SVAR and local projections

    SVAR IRF definition

    Consider a reduced-form VAR $$y_t=\mu+\sum_{i=1}^{L}A_i y_{t-i}+\epsilon_t,$$ where $y_t$ includes (at minimum) an exchange rate and a price measure, and typically additional variables needed to represent the relevant information set. A structural interpretation imposes a decomposition $$\epsilon_t = S u_t,\quad E[u_tu_t']=I,$$ where $u_t$ includes an identified exchange-rate shock $u^e_t$.

    Let $\text{IRF}_{p,e}(j)$ denote the response of $p$ at horizon $j$ to a normalized exchange-rate shock. IRF-based cumulative pass-through is defined as $$\text{ERPT}^{IRF}(h)=\sum_{j=0}^{h}\text{IRF}_{p,e}(j).$$ This object is structurally interpretable only relative to the identification scheme that defines $u^e_t$. Altering identification restrictions changes the shock, and therefore changes the estimand, not only its uncertainty.

    Local projections as IRF estimators

    Local projections estimate horizon-specific responses without imposing that the entire dynamic system is correctly summarized by a single finite-order VAR recursion. For each horizon $h$, one may estimate $$p_{t+h}-p_{t-1}=a_h+b_h s_t+\pi_h' q_t+\nu_{t+h},$$ where $s_t$ is either an observed $\Delta e_t$ or an identified shock proxy, and $q_t$ includes lags and controls intended to approximate the relevant information set. The local projection coefficient $b_h$ is interpreted as an IRF coefficient only if $s_t$ is a valid shock measure and the conditional mean restriction $E[\nu_{t+h}\mid s_t,q_t]=0$ is credible.

    A defining feature is that inference is sensitive to overlapping forecast errors when $p_{t+h}-p_{t-1}$ is used, which generally requires robust covariance estimation. Jordà (2005) is a canonical reference for local projection IRFs and their inference properties (Jordà, 2005).

    Why IRF-based ERPT differs from non-IRF ERPT

    In general, regression-based $B(h)$ and IRF-based $\text{ERPT}^{IRF}(h)$ coincide only under restrictive conditions. A sufficient set of conditions includes: a stable linear data-generating process, correct specification of the information set, equivalence between observed exchange-rate innovations and the structural exchange-rate shock, and absence of confounding contemporaneous shocks that jointly move $\Delta e_t$ and $\Delta p_t$. When these conditions fail, the gap between the two methods is not a statistical artifact; it reflects different estimands.

    The primary source of divergence is shock contamination. An observed exchange-rate change can be decomposed as $$\Delta e_t = a\varepsilon^e_t + b\varepsilon^m_t + c\varepsilon^{rp}_t + d\varepsilon^{tot}_t + \cdots,$$ where $\varepsilon^m_t$ is a monetary-policy-related shock, $\varepsilon^{rp}_t$ is a risk-premium shock, and $\varepsilon^{tot}_t$ is a terms-of-trade shock. A regression of $\Delta p_t$ on $\Delta e_t$ estimates a response to the composite regressor, while an IRF estimates the response to one component defined by identification restrictions.

    A second source is omitted-state bias in dynamic systems. If $y_t$ omits a state variable that drives both the exchange rate and pricing (for example, global commodity prices for commodity exporters, or global risk factors for financially open economies), the identified “exchange-rate shock” can partially proxy the omitted state, and the IRF can attribute propagation to the exchange rate that is actually driven by the omitted factor. Regression-based approaches can suffer analogous omitted-variable bias, but system approaches can additionally change the shock definition through the omitted variable’s effect on the reduced-form residual covariance structure.

    A third source is normalization and scaling. In IRF work, a one-standard-deviation shock is not equivalent to a one-percent depreciation shock unless the exchange-rate innovation is scaled accordingly. Reported pass-through paths can therefore differ mechanically if normalization conventions differ, even when underlying estimates are similar.

    Error sources and required controls

    Endogeneity and simultaneity

    A structural interpretation requires separation of exchange-rate innovations from contemporaneous shocks that directly affect prices. If import prices respond contemporaneously to foreign-currency marginal costs and the exchange rate responds contemporaneously to the same global shocks, then $E[u_t\Delta e_t]\neq 0$ is expected. Including controls such as commodity prices or foreign producer prices helps only if they are adequate proxies and are measured without significant error; otherwise, omitted-variable bias remains.

    SVAR identification is an explicit attempt to address simultaneity, but it transfers the burden to the credibility of restrictions. External-instrument identification is one such approach, but inference becomes sensitive to instrument strength. Weak instruments can produce unreliable impulse responses and misleading uncertainty bands, and these issues are central in the SVAR-IV literature (see Montiel Olea, Stock & Watson for SVAR-IV inference).

    Measurement and aggregation

    ERPT depends on the price object. Border prices incorporate exchange-rate translation and foreign marginal costs; CPI incorporates distribution margins, taxes, and non-tradables. Aggregation can attenuate measured pass-through through compositional shifts, changing expenditure weights, and quality adjustments, all of which can create a low measured ERPT even when micro-level pass-through is higher.

    Exchange-rate measurement also matters. Bilateral rates, effective rates, and invoicing-currency rates correspond to different economic exposures. When invoicing is concentrated in a dominant currency, the dominant-currency exchange rate can be more relevant than the bilateral rate for pricing and pass-through. This is formalized in the dominant currency paradigm (see Gopinath et al., 2020).

    Dynamic specification errors

    In distributed-lag regressions, lag length $K$ affects both bias and variance. If adjustment is slow and $K$ is short, $B(h)$ truncates the response and understates medium-run pass-through. If $K$ is long relative to the sample length, the regressor matrix becomes ill-conditioned, lag coefficients become imprecise, and cumulative sums can exhibit artificial oscillations.

    In VAR/SVAR settings, lag order $L$ and variable ordering/selection affect both reduced-form fit and the implied moving-average representation. Underfitting lags biases IRFs by forcing persistent dynamics into insufficient state dimension; overfitting lags can increase noise and destabilize the estimated companion matrix. Local projections reduce reliance on a single recursion but shift risk to horizon-by-horizon misspecification and to the handling of overlapping forecast errors.

    Structural breaks and regime dependence

    ERPT is not generally stable across inflation regimes, policy frameworks, or large shifts in trade structure. A coefficient that is stable in a low-inflation regime may not be stable in a high-inflation regime, and estimates that pool regimes implicitly average heterogeneous dynamics. Theoretical mechanisms linking inflation persistence to pricing decisions motivate this instability (see Taylor, 2000).

    Economic reasoning and small open economy configuration

    I think this section yields the most important part of the configuration, model configuration should absolutely reflect the economic environment. Estimating ERPT in a small open economy requires particular attention to external drivers and the distinction between domestic and global shocks, because the exchange rate often reacts strongly to external conditions that are not controlled by domestic policy. If these external drivers are omitted, exchange-rate innovations in the model will embed foreign demand, global risk, and commodity price movements, and estimated ERPT will reflect both exchange-rate transmission and the direct effects of external shocks on prices.

    In a small open economy, a defensible system often separates foreign variables (treated as exogenous or block-exogenous) from domestic variables. A block-exogenous structure may be represented as $$\begin{pmatrix} y_t^f \\ y_t^d \end{pmatrix} = \mu + \sum_{i=1}^{L} \begin{pmatrix} A_{ff,i} & 0 \\ A_{df,i} & A_{dd,i} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} y_{t-i}^f \\ y_{t-i}^d \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} \epsilon_t^f \\ \epsilon_t^d \end{pmatrix},$$ where $y^f_t$ captures foreign conditions and $y^d_t$ captures domestic conditions. If the block-exogeneity restriction is appropriate, it prevents domestic shocks from being misinterpreted as drivers of foreign variables and clarifies the interpretation of domestic exchange-rate shocks.

    The selection of $y^f_t$ and the definition of the exchange-rate shock require economic justification. Commodity exporters often require commodity prices in the foreign block because they affect both the exchange rate and domestic inflation directly through input costs and fiscal channels. Financially open economies often require a global risk measure and foreign interest rates because they affect the exchange rate via capital flows and risk premia, and they can directly affect domestic inflation via imported financing conditions.

    Monetary regimes matter for identification. Under a credible inflation-targeting regime, an exchange-rate depreciation can trigger a policy response that dampens subsequent inflation; under weaker credibility, policy accommodation can amplify inflation dynamics. If the model does not include a policy reaction variable, the estimated ERPT may mechanically incorporate policy feedback in an uncontrolled manner, potentially producing estimates that are not comparable across regimes.

    Invoicing structure and trade integration are additional sources of heterogeneity. In economies where imports are invoiced in a dominant currency, the relevant exchange-rate measure for border prices may be the domestic currency per dominant currency, not per bilateral partner currency, and failure to use the economically relevant exchange rate can induce apparent low pass-through in bilateral regressions even when pass-through is high with respect to the invoicing-currency rate (see Gopinath et al., 2020).

    Practical requirements for credible ERPT estimates

    A credible empirical specification should state the price margin (import prices, PPI, CPI), the exchange-rate concept (bilateral, effective, invoicing-currency), and the shock definition. For IRF-based ERPT, the identification restrictions or external instruments should be documented with sufficient detail to allow replication, and normalization must be explicit. For regression-based ERPT, the interpretation should be limited to conditional responses unless a shock identification strategy is embedded.

    When comparing IRF-based ERPT to regression-based ERPT, the most common error is treating a regression-based $B(h)$ as if it were $\text{ERPT}^{IRF}(h)$. The two can be numerically similar in some settings, but similarity does not imply equivalence of estimands. Differences should be interpreted as evidence about shock composition, omitted states, or model restrictions, not as a failure of one estimator in isolation.
