1  Volatility Clustering

Financial returns exhibit time-varying volatility: large shocks tend to be followed by large shocks (of either sign), and small by small. Engle (1982) formalised this as Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity (ARCH), where the conditional variance depends on past squared innovations.

2  The ARCH(q) Model

ARCH(q) Specification
$$y_t = \mu_t + \varepsilon_t, \quad \varepsilon_t = \sigma_t z_t, \quad z_t \sim \text{iid}(0,1)$$ $$\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha_1 \varepsilon_{t-1}^2 + \alpha_2 \varepsilon_{t-2}^2 + \cdots + \alpha_q \varepsilon_{t-q}^2$$

with $\omega > 0$ and $\alpha_i \geq 0$ ensuring positivity. The process is covariance stationary if $\sum_{i=1}^q \alpha_i < 1$, in which case the unconditional variance is $\text{Var}(\varepsilon_t) = \omega/(1 - \sum \alpha_i)$.

3  Properties

Despite zero conditional autocorrelation ($\text{Cov}(\varepsilon_t, \varepsilon_{t-k}) = 0$), the squared process $\varepsilon_t^2$ exhibits positive autocorrelation. In fact, $\varepsilon_t^2$ follows an AR($q$):

$$\varepsilon_t^2 = \omega + \sum_{i=1}^q \alpha_i \varepsilon_{t-i}^2 + v_t, \quad v_t = \varepsilon_t^2 - \sigma_t^2$$

where $v_t$ is a martingale difference. ARCH processes also exhibit excess kurtosis (fat tails):

$$\kappa = \frac{\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_t^4]}{(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_t^2])^2} > 3 \quad \text{(for Gaussian } z_t\text{)}$$

4  MLE for ARCH

Under Gaussian $z_t$, the conditional log-likelihood is:

ARCH Log-Likelihood
$$\ell = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\left[\ln(2\pi) + \ln(\sigma_t^2) + \frac{\varepsilon_t^2}{\sigma_t^2}\right]$$

Maximised numerically over $(\omega, \alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_q)$. Quasi-MLE (QMLE) remains consistent under non-Gaussian $z_t$ with correct specification of the conditional mean and variance, though standard errors require the Bollerslev-Wooldridge sandwich correction.

5  Testing for ARCH Effects

Engle's (1982) LM test regresses squared residuals $\hat{\varepsilon}_t^2$ on a constant and $q$ lags:

$$\hat{\varepsilon}_t^2 = \beta_0 + \beta_1\hat{\varepsilon}_{t-1}^2 + \cdots + \beta_q\hat{\varepsilon}_{t-q}^2 + e_t$$

Under $H_0: \alpha_1 = \cdots = \alpha_q = 0$, the test statistic $TR^2 \sim \chi^2_q$ where $R^2$ is from this auxiliary regression.

6  Limitations

ARCH($q$) requires many parameters to capture persistent volatility. Financial volatility typically needs $q \geq 10$. The model treats positive and negative shocks symmetrically, ignoring the leverage effect (negative shocks tend to increase volatility more than positive shocks of the same magnitude). These limitations motivate GARCH.
