1  The Likelihood Principle

Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) selects the parameter vector that makes the observed data most probable. Given a sample $\{y_1, \ldots, y_T\}$ with joint density $f(\mathbf{y}; \boldsymbol{\theta})$ where $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^p$, the likelihood function treats the density as a function of the parameters:

Likelihood Function
$$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}; \mathbf{y}) = f(\mathbf{y}; \boldsymbol{\theta})$$

The log-likelihood, $\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \ln \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$, is preferred for computational convenience. Under independent observations:

$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \ln f(y_t; \boldsymbol{\theta})$$

2  The MLE

MLE Definition
$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{ML}} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \Theta} \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta})$$

The first-order condition defines the score equations:

Score Equations
$$\mathbf{s}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \frac{\partial \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta})}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{\partial \ln f(y_t; \boldsymbol{\theta})}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}} = \mathbf{0}$$

3  Asymptotic Properties

Under standard regularity conditions (Cramér, 1946), as $T \to \infty$:

Consistency: $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{ML}} \xrightarrow{p} \boldsymbol{\theta}_0$. The information inequality ensures the true parameter uniquely maximises the expected log-likelihood.

Asymptotic normality:

Asymptotic Distribution
$$\sqrt{T}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{ML}} - \boldsymbol{\theta}_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\left(\mathbf{0}, \mathcal{I}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_0)^{-1}\right)$$

where $\mathcal{I}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is the Fisher information matrix:

Fisher Information
$$\mathcal{I}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = -\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial^2 \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta})}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta} \partial \boldsymbol{\theta}'}\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[\mathbf{s}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\mathbf{s}(\boldsymbol{\theta})'\right]$$

Efficiency: MLE achieves the Cramér-Rao lower bound. No consistent estimator has smaller asymptotic variance.

4  The Trinity of Tests

For testing $H_0: \mathbf{h}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathbf{0}$ (with $q$ restrictions):

Likelihood Ratio (LR):

$$\text{LR} = 2[\ell(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) - \ell(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\theta}})] \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_q$$

Wald:

$$W = \mathbf{h}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})'[\mathbf{H}\hat{\mathcal{I}}^{-1}\mathbf{H}']^{-1}\mathbf{h}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_q$$

Lagrange Multiplier (Score):

$$\text{LM} = \mathbf{s}(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\theta}})'\tilde{\mathcal{I}}^{-1}\mathbf{s}(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_q$$

where $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ is the unrestricted MLE, $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ is the restricted MLE, and $\mathbf{H} = \partial \mathbf{h}/\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}'$.

5  MLE for the Normal Linear Model

Under $\mathbf{u} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2\mathbf{I}_T)$:

$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\beta}, \sigma^2) = -\frac{T}{2}\ln(2\pi) - \frac{T}{2}\ln(\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})'(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})$$

Maximising over $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ yields $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{\text{ML}} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{y}$, identical to OLS. The ML variance estimator is $\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{ML}} = \hat{\mathbf{u}}'\hat{\mathbf{u}}/T$ (biased, with divisor $T$ instead of $T-k$).

6  Conditional MLE for Time Series

For dependent observations (e.g., AR, GARCH models), the joint density factorises via the chain rule of probability:

Conditional Decomposition
$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \ln f(y_1; \boldsymbol{\theta}) + \sum_{t=2}^{T} \ln f(y_t \mid y_{t-1}, \ldots, y_1; \boldsymbol{\theta})$$

The conditional MLE drops the awkward first term and maximises $\sum_{t=p+1}^{T} \ln f(y_t \mid \mathcal{F}_{t-1}; \boldsymbol{\theta})$ where $p$ is the model order. Under stationarity and ergodicity, conditional MLE retains full asymptotic efficiency.

7  Information Criteria

Model selection among non-nested specifications uses penalised likelihoods:

$$\text{AIC} = -2\ell(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) + 2p, \qquad \text{BIC} = -2\ell(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) + p \ln T$$

AIC is asymptotically efficient (minimises prediction error); BIC is consistent (selects the true model with probability approaching 1 as $T \to \infty$).
