1  Moment Conditions

The Generalized Method of Moments (Hansen, 1982) builds on a set of population moment conditions. Given data $\{w_t\}_{t=1}^T$ and a parameter vector $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^p$, suppose there exist $q \geq p$ moment conditions:

Population Moment Conditions
$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{g}(w_t, \boldsymbol{\theta}_0)] = \mathbf{0}$$

where $\mathbf{g}: \mathbb{R}^d \times \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}^q$ is a known function and $\boldsymbol{\theta}_0$ is the true parameter. The sample analogue is:

$$\bar{\mathbf{g}}_T(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\mathbf{g}(w_t, \boldsymbol{\theta})$$

2  The GMM Estimator

When $q > p$ (overidentified), $\bar{\mathbf{g}}_T(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathbf{0}$ cannot be solved exactly. GMM minimises a quadratic form:

GMM Objective
$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{GMM}} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \, \bar{\mathbf{g}}_T(\boldsymbol{\theta})'\mathbf{W}_T\bar{\mathbf{g}}_T(\boldsymbol{\theta})$$

where $\mathbf{W}_T$ is a $q \times q$ positive definite weighting matrix. The first-order conditions are:

$$\left(\frac{\partial \bar{\mathbf{g}}_T}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}'}\right)'\mathbf{W}_T\bar{\mathbf{g}}_T(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) = \mathbf{0}$$

3  Optimal Weighting

GMM is consistent for any positive definite $\mathbf{W}_T$, but efficiency depends on the choice. The asymptotically optimal weight is:

Efficient Weighting
$$\mathbf{W}^*_T = \hat{\mathbf{S}}^{-1}, \quad \mathbf{S} = \lim_{T\to\infty} \text{Var}\left(\sqrt{T}\bar{\mathbf{g}}_T(\boldsymbol{\theta}_0)\right)$$

The efficient GMM (two-step or iterated) first estimates $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ with $\mathbf{W} = \mathbf{I}$, constructs $\hat{\mathbf{S}}$ from the residuals, then re-estimates with $\mathbf{W} = \hat{\mathbf{S}}^{-1}$. The asymptotic distribution is:

Asymptotic Distribution
$$\sqrt{T}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} - \boldsymbol{\theta}_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\left(\mathbf{0}, (\mathbf{D}'\mathbf{S}^{-1}\mathbf{D})^{-1}\right)$$

where $\mathbf{D} = \mathbb{E}[\partial \mathbf{g}/\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}']$ is the $q \times p$ Jacobian.

4  Overidentification Test

Hansen's (1982) J-test evaluates whether the moment conditions are jointly satisfied:

J-Statistic
$$J_T = T\,\bar{\mathbf{g}}_T(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})'\hat{\mathbf{S}}^{-1}\bar{\mathbf{g}}_T(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{q-p}$$

Rejection indicates misspecification: either the model is wrong or instruments are invalid.

5  GMM as Unifying Framework

GMM nests many estimators as special cases. OLS corresponds to moments $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}_t(y_t - \mathbf{x}_t'\boldsymbol{\beta})] = \mathbf{0}$. IV/2SLS uses instrument moments $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{z}_t(y_t - \mathbf{x}_t'\boldsymbol{\beta})] = \mathbf{0}$. MLE solves score conditions $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{s}(\boldsymbol{\theta})] = \mathbf{0}$. The Euler equation approach in asset pricing (Hansen and Singleton, 1982) uses $\mathbb{E}[\beta(C_{t+1}/C_t)^{-\gamma}R_{t+1} - 1] = 0$.

6  Practical Implementation

The HAC estimator for $\mathbf{S}$ when moments exhibit serial correlation follows Newey-West:

$$\hat{\mathbf{S}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{\Gamma}}_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{m}\left(1 - \frac{j}{m+1}\right)(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Gamma}}_j + \hat{\boldsymbol{\Gamma}}_j')$$

where $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Gamma}}_j = T^{-1}\sum_{t=j+1}^T \mathbf{g}_t\mathbf{g}_{t-j}'$ with $\mathbf{g}_t = \mathbf{g}(w_t, \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})$.

7  Weak Instruments

When instruments are weakly correlated with endogenous regressors, GMM can exhibit severe finite-sample bias and size distortion. Stock and Yogo (2005) provide critical values. The first-stage $F$-statistic is the standard diagnostic: $F < 10$ signals concern. Weak-instrument-robust tests (Anderson-Rubin, conditional likelihood ratio) maintain correct size regardless of instrument strength.
