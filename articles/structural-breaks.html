1  The Break Problem

A structural break is a discrete, permanent change in the parameters of a model at an unknown date $T_b$. Ignoring breaks leads to biased estimates, invalid forecasts, and misleading inference. The model with $m$ breaks at dates $T_1, \ldots, T_m$:

$$y_t = \mathbf{x}_t'\boldsymbol{\beta}_j + u_t, \quad T_{j-1} < t \leq T_j, \quad j = 1, \ldots, m+1$$

2  Chow Test (Known Break Date)

When $T_b$ is known, the Chow (1960) test compares restricted (pooled) and unrestricted (split-sample) regressions:

Chow F-Statistic
$$F = \frac{(\text{RSS}_R - \text{RSS}_1 - \text{RSS}_2)/k}{(\text{RSS}_1 + \text{RSS}_2)/(T - 2k)} \sim F_{k, T-2k}$$

3  CUSUM and CUSUM-of-Squares

Brown, Durbin and Evans (1975) proposed monitoring cumulative sums of recursive residuals $w_t$:

CUSUM
$$W_t = \frac{1}{\hat{\sigma}}\sum_{j=k+1}^{t}w_j$$

Under stability, $W_t$ fluctuates around zero within significance bands $\pm c\sqrt{T-k}$ (where $c$ depends on the significance level). Departure signals instability. The CUSUM-of-squares uses $\sum w_j^2$ to detect variance shifts.

4  Andrews (1993) SupF Test

When the break date is unknown, Andrews (1993) proposes the supremum of Chow statistics over a trimmed range $[\pi_0 T, (1-\pi_0)T]$:

SupF
$$\text{SupF} = \sup_{\pi \in [\pi_0, 1-\pi_0]} F(\pi T)$$

Critical values are non-standard (supremum of a chi-squared process) and depend on $\pi_0$ (typically 0.15) and the number of parameters. Hansen (1997) provides $p$-values via simulation.

5  Bai-Perron (1998, 2003) Multiple Breaks

Bai and Perron developed a comprehensive framework for estimating and testing multiple structural breaks. The break dates are estimated by global minimisation of the sum of squared residuals:

$$(\hat{T}_1, \ldots, \hat{T}_m) = \arg\min_{T_1, \ldots, T_m} \sum_{j=1}^{m+1}\sum_{t=T_{j-1}+1}^{T_j}(y_t - \mathbf{x}_t'\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_j)^2$$

Efficient computation uses the dynamic programming algorithm of Bai and Perron (2003). Sequential testing determines the number of breaks: the $\text{SupF}(l+1|l)$ test evaluates whether adding an $(l+1)$-th break significantly improves fit given $l$ breaks. Confidence intervals for break dates use the asymptotic distribution of $\hat{T}_j$.

6  Implications for Unit Root Testing

Perron (1989) showed that unaccounted structural breaks bias ADF tests towards non-rejection of the unit root null. A stationary series with a level shift or trend break can mimic a unit root process. The Zivot-Andrews (1992) test endogenises the break date, and the Lee-Strazicich (2003) test allows breaks under both null and alternative.
