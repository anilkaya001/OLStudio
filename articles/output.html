This article estimates Türkiye’s output gap from 1995 Q1 through 2025 Q4 using seven established methods — Hamilton (2018) regression filter, Hodrick-Prescott (λ = 1600), Baxter-King band-pass, Christiano-Fitzgerald band-pass, Beveridge-Nelson decomposition, a UC/Kalman local-linear-trend model, and a quadratic deterministic trend — combined through skill-weighted Bayesian Model Averaging via leave-one-out cross-validation RMSE (Yao et al. 2018; Timmermann 2006). The LOO-stacked ensemble places the output gap at -0.49% in 2025 Q4 (IQR: [-3.07%, -0.31%]), consistent with a demand-compression cycle following the post-2023 monetary tightening episode. The UC/Kalman filter receives the dominant ensemble weight (67.7%), having achieved the lowest LOO-RMSE of 0.17 percentage points.

1  The Output Gap

The output gap — the percentage deviation of actual real GDP from its potential level — is among the most policy-relevant concepts in macroeconomics. A positive gap signals demand in excess of productive capacity (inflationary pressure); a negative gap denotes slack (disinflationary forces). For Türkiye, an economy characterised by large external shocks, structural breaks, and persistent real exchange-rate volatility, the choice of detrending method is far from neutral. Rather than selecting a single filter, the approach here pools seven complementary methods through a skill-weighted ensemble — producing a richer picture of cyclical uncertainty than any single estimate can provide.

2  Methods

2.1  Hamilton (2018) Regression Filter

Hamilton (2018) demonstrated that the HP filter produces spurious cycles, suffers from severe endpoint bias, and imposes economically implausible priors. He proposed the OLS projection

Hamilton Regression
$$y_{t+h} = \alpha + \beta_1 y_t + \beta_2 y_{t-1} + \beta_3 y_{t-2} + \beta_4 y_{t-3} + v_{t+h}$$

with $h = 8$ quarters. The residual $\hat v_{t+h}$ is the cycle. Because regressors are predetermined, the cycle is a valid stationary series under mild conditions — no spurious co-integration.

2.2  Hodrick-Prescott, Band-Pass & BN Filters

The HP filter ($\lambda = 1600$) serves as the standard benchmark. Baxter-King (1999) and Christiano-Fitzgerald (2003) band-pass filters isolate fluctuations with periodicity in $[6,\,32]$ quarters. The Beveridge-Nelson (1981) decomposition defines potential as the long-run forecast from an AR(1) fitted to $\Delta \ln y_t$, selected by AIC.

2.3  Unobserved Components / Kalman Filter

The UC model decomposes log-GDP into a local linear trend $\mu_t$ and a stochastic damped cycle $\psi_t$, estimated jointly by maximum likelihood via the Kalman smoother. The estimated cycle period is approximately 10.4 quarters.

2.4  Skill-Weighted BMA Ensemble

Each method receives a weight proportional to the inverse of its squared leave-one-out RMSE, with a 5 % floor to maintain diversity. The UC/Kalman filter, with the lowest RMSE, receives 67.7 %; the Beveridge-Nelson decomposition receives 11.7 %.

The 2025 Q4 estimates range from −4.79 % (Quadratic Trend) to +1.22 % (Baxter-King, subject to endpoint imprecision). The LOO-stacked ensemble places the gap at -0.49 % (IQR: [-3.07 %, -0.31 %]). The correlation matrix confirms that the Beveridge-Nelson decomposition diverges from the rest of the ensemble, providing genuine diversification. A negative gap of this magnitude is consistent with declining core inflation, rising unemployment, and scope for accommodative monetary policy.
