1  Regime-Dependent Dynamics

Hamilton (1989) introduced the Markov-switching model to capture discrete shifts in the behaviour of economic time series. The model allows parameters to depend on an unobserved state variable $S_t \in \{1, 2, \ldots, M\}$:

MS Model
$$y_t = \boldsymbol{\mu}_{S_t} + \sum_{j=1}^p \boldsymbol{\phi}_{j,S_t} y_{t-j} + \sigma_{S_t}\varepsilon_t, \quad \varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

2  Transition Probabilities

The state follows a first-order Markov chain with transition matrix:

Transition Matrix
$$\mathbf{P} = \begin{pmatrix} p_{11} & p_{12} & \cdots & p_{1M} \\ p_{21} & p_{22} & \cdots & p_{2M} \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots \\ p_{M1} & p_{M2} & \cdots & p_{MM} \end{pmatrix}$$

where $p_{ij} = \Pr(S_t = j \mid S_{t-1} = i)$ with $\sum_j p_{ij} = 1$. The expected duration in regime $i$ is $1/(1-p_{ii})$.

3  Hamilton Filter

The filtered probability $\Pr(S_t = j \mid y_1, \ldots, y_t; \boldsymbol{\theta})$ is computed recursively:

Prediction
$$\Pr(S_t = j \mid \mathcal{Y}_{t-1}) = \sum_{i=1}^M p_{ij} \Pr(S_{t-1} = i \mid \mathcal{Y}_{t-1})$$
Update
$$\Pr(S_t = j \mid \mathcal{Y}_t) = \frac{f(y_t \mid S_t = j, \mathcal{Y}_{t-1})\Pr(S_t = j \mid \mathcal{Y}_{t-1})}{\sum_{k=1}^M f(y_t \mid S_t = k, \mathcal{Y}_{t-1})\Pr(S_t = k \mid \mathcal{Y}_{t-1})}$$

The log-likelihood is $\ell = \sum_t \ln\left[\sum_j f(y_t \mid S_t=j)P(S_t=j \mid \mathcal{Y}_{t-1})\right]$, maximised via EM or direct numerical optimisation.

4  Kim Smoother

Kim (1994) developed the backward recursion for smoothed probabilities $\Pr(S_t = j \mid \mathcal{Y}_T)$ using all available information, paralleling the Kalman smoother. Smoothed probabilities are sharper than filtered ones and better identify regime timing.

5  Applications

Business cycles: Hamilton (1989) identified expansions and recessions in US GDP growth as distinct regimes with different means and variances. Volatility: Switching GARCH models allow the volatility process itself to shift between regimes. Monetary policy: Regime shifts in Taylor rule parameters detect changes in central bank behaviour. Exchange rates: Switching models capture the alternation between fixed and floating regimes or tranquil vs. crisis periods.

6  Testing for Regime Switching

Testing $H_0$: single regime vs. $H_1$: multiple regimes faces the Davies (1977) problem: nuisance parameters ($p_{ij}$, regime-specific parameters) are unidentified under $H_0$, invalidating the standard LR distribution. Hansen (1992) proposed bounding the $p$-value; Carrasco, Hu and Ploberger (2014) developed optimal tests. Garcia (1998) provides simulation-based critical values.
