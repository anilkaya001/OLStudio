Central Bank Communication as Cheap Talk

Central bank communication is inherently "coarse" in the short term because strategic bias prevents precise truth-telling—banks cannot shift average expectations, only distort them. However, in a repeated relationship, credibility functions as a structural asset: if outcomes are verifiable and the bank values future influence enough, the long-term cost of losing trust disciplines the bank, allowing for fully truthful communication despite their bias.

More recent models incorporate private information, where the central bank observes economic shocks that the public does not. This creates a cheap talk game: the central bank sends signals about the economy, but the public knows the bank has incentives to manipulate those signals to achieve favorable expectations.

In equilibrium, communication becomes coarse: the central bank can only credibly convey broad ranges of information rather than precise details. The degree of credibility depends on the alignment of preferences between the central bank and the public, measured by the bias parameter in the loss function.

1) A minimal model of strategic communication

Let $\theta \in [0,1]$ be an economic state that the central bank observes and the public does not. The central bank sends a message $m$ and the public chooses an action $a$ interpreted as a policy relevant expectation, such as expected inflation or a forecasted policy stance. Messages are costless and do not directly enter payoffs.

A canonical quadratic specification is $$U^{P}(\theta,a) = (a - \theta)^2,$$ $$U^{CB}(\theta,a) = (a - \theta - b)^2,$$ where $b>0$ is the central bank bias, the wedge between the action the public prefers and the action the central bank prefers in each state. The public minimizes $U^{P}$ and the central bank minimizes $U^{CB}$.

Interpretation. The central bank is not irrational or malicious in this model. It is simply optimizing a different quadratic loss, so it would like expectations and actions to be shifted by $b$ relative to what the public would choose if it knew $\theta$. The strategic problem is that the message itself is not verifiable at the time it is sent.

2) Why equilibrium communication is coarse

The public chooses an optimal action given its posterior belief about $\theta$ after receiving $m$. In the quadratic case, the optimal action is the posterior mean: $$a(m) = \mathbb{E}[\theta \mid m].$$ Therefore the only way the central bank influences the action is by shaping the posterior through its message rule.

Lemma 1. For any equilibrium message rule and any induced posterior, the unconditional mean action equals the unconditional mean state: $$\mathbb{E}[a] = \mathbb{E}[\theta].$$

Proof. Using $a(m)=\mathbb{E}[\theta\mid m]$ and the law of iterated expectations, $$\mathbb{E}[a] = \mathbb{E}\big[\mathbb{E}[\theta\mid m]\big] = \mathbb{E}[\theta]. \quad \square$$

Lemma 1 is often underappreciated: a biased sender cannot move the unconditional mean action in this class of models. What the bias can do is distort how expectations respond to states, by pooling some states and separating others. This is the formal sense in which communication becomes coarse.

3) The static partition logic and an explicit existence bound

In the uniform quadratic benchmark, informative equilibria take the form of monotone partitions of $[0,1]$ into $L$ intervals. If the public believes $\theta$ lies in $(t_{i-1},t_i]$, it chooses $$a_i = \mathbb{E}[\theta\mid \theta\in(t_{i-1},t_i]] = \frac{t_{i-1}+t_i}{2}.$$

Proposition 1. Fix $L \ge 2$. In the uniform quadratic model there exists an $L$ step partition equilibrium with cutoffs $$0=t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_L=1$$ if and only if $$b < \frac{1}{2L(L - 1)}.$$ Moreover the equilibrium cutoffs satisfy $$t_i = i\,t_1 + 2b\,i(i - 1), \qquad t_1 = \frac{1 - 2b\,L(L - 1)}{L}.$$

Proof. Consider a candidate partition and the induced actions $a_i=(t_{i-1}+t_i)/2$. At each interior cutoff $t_i$, type $\theta=t_i$ must be indifferent between sending $m_i$ and $m_{i+1}$: $$(a_i - t_i - b)^2 = (a_{i+1} - t_i - b)^2.$$ Since $a_{i+1} > a_i$, this implies $$t_i + b = \frac{a_i + a_{i+1}}{2} = \frac{t_{i-1}+2t_i+t_{i+1}}{4}.$$ Rearranging yields the recursion $$t_{i+1} - t_i = (t_i - t_{i-1}) + 4b.$$ Define interval lengths $\Delta_i = t_i - t_{i-1}$. Then $\Delta_{i+1} = \Delta_i + 4b$, hence $$\Delta_i = \Delta_1 + 4b(i - 1).$$ Summing lengths gives $$t_i = \sum_{j=1}^{i}\Delta_j = i\Delta_1 + 4b\sum_{j=1}^{i}(j - 1) = i\Delta_1 + 2b\,i(i - 1).$$ Since $t_0=0$, we identify $t_1=\Delta_1$ and obtain the claimed expression. Imposing $t_L=1$ gives $$1 = L t_1 + 2b\,L(L - 1), \quad \Rightarrow \quad t_1 = \frac{1 - 2b\,L(L - 1)}{L}.$$ Monotonicity requires $\Delta_1=t_1>0$, which is equivalent to $b < 1/(2L(L - 1))$. Conversely, if this holds, then all $\Delta_i$ are positive and the constructed cutoffs define an equilibrium partition. $\square$

Proposition 1 is the clearest statement of the credibility constraint in the static benchmark. When bias increases, the maximal feasible $L$ falls sharply, so the public receives a cruder description of the state. In that sense, an aggressive bias can destroy the very informational channel the central bank relies on to transmit policy.

4) Committee decision making and why dispersion matters

When policy is set by a committee, the informational problem is not only the average view. The dispersion of views changes how the public interprets the same average statement, because dispersion is a proxy for uncertainty about the internal mapping from states to actions. If only an average target is announced while disagreement is concealed, the public rationally assigns probability to a range of internal reaction functions. In a cheap talk environment, that additional uncertainty typically tightens credibility constraints, because the receiver cannot condition as finely on hidden heterogeneity.

Practical implication. Credibility is not achieved by publishing a mean. It is achieved by publishing a mean together with enough structure that the public can infer how the mean was produced, including the dispersion that disciplines future revisions.

5) Persistent bias as an effective instrument

The phrase "persistent bias" is not merely a descriptive label. It is a structural assumption that changes incentives because it creates a repeated relationship between the central bank and the public. The public forms expectations today, learns realized outcomes later, and uses those outcomes to update its view of the central bank. When this loop repeats, credibility becomes an intertemporal asset.

The key question is not whether the central bank is biased. The key question is how much distance can be sustained while still keeping communication informative enough to shape expectations in a disciplined way. The cleanest way to make this precise is to embed the static environment in an infinite horizon repeated game with verifiable outcomes.

5.1 A repeated game with observable outcomes

Time is $t=0,1,2,\ldots$. Each period, nature draws $\theta_t \sim \mathrm{Unif}[0,1]$ independently. The central bank observes $\theta_t$, sends a message $m_t$, and the public chooses $a_t$. After the action, the realized state becomes observable, so deviations from an announced reporting rule can be detected. The central bank discounts the future with factor $\delta \in (0,1)$.

Proposition 2. In the repeated game described above, a truth telling equilibrium exists under a simple grim trigger strategy provided $$b \le \sqrt{\frac{\delta}{12(1 - \delta)}}.$$ Equivalently, for a given bias $b$, truth telling is sustainable if $$\delta \ge \frac{12b^2}{1 + 12b^2}.$$

Proof. Consider the strategy profile: on the equilibrium path the central bank reports truthfully and the public sets $a_t=\theta_t$. After any detected deviation, the public ignores all future messages and plays the babbling action $a_t=\mathbb{E}[\theta_t]=1/2$ forever.

First compute payoffs on the equilibrium path. With $a_t=\theta_t$, the central bank per period loss is $(\theta_t - \theta_t - b)^2=b^2$.

Now bound the maximal one period gain from deviating. In a single period, the best possible action for the central bank would be $a_t=\theta_t+b$, yielding loss $0$. Hence the largest improvement relative to truthful play is at most $b^2$ in that period.

Next compute the per period loss under babbling. With $a_t=1/2$, $$\mathbb{E}\big[(1/2 - \theta_t - b)^2\big] = \mathbb{E}\big[(1/2 - \theta_t)^2\big] + b^2 = \frac{1}{12} + b^2,$$ since $\theta_t$ is uniform on $[0,1]$ and $\mathbb{E}[(\theta_t - 1/2)^2]=\mathrm{Var}(\theta_t)=1/12$. Therefore the incremental per period penalty from punishment, relative to truthful play, equals $1/12$. The discounted value of this incremental penalty from next period onward is $$\frac{\delta}{1 - \delta}\cdot \frac{1}{12}.$$

The one deviation principle implies that truth telling is an equilibrium if the maximal current gain is no larger than the discounted future loss: $$b^2 \le \frac{\delta}{1 - \delta}\cdot \frac{1}{12}.$$ Rearranging yields the stated conditions. $\square$

Proposition 2 gives a mathematically explicit meaning to "how distant can the central bank be." For a fixed discount factor, there is a maximal sustainable bias. If the bias exceeds this bound, the temptation to exploit the public in the current period dominates the value of future credibility, and the equilibrium reverts to coarse or even uninformative communication.

Why persistence is effective. The same persistence that creates temptation also creates leverage. When outcomes become observable, the future cost of losing credibility is a discipline device. As $\delta$ rises, the credibility asset becomes more valuable, so even a biased central bank can sustain informative communication. This is the core mechanism behind dynamic results in the strategic transmission literature.

5.2 A design implication stated as a distance rule

If shaping expectations is interpreted as enabling the public to condition actions on the state, then the relevant performance statistic is informational, not directional. A standard measure is the expected posterior variance: $$\mathbb{E}\big[\mathrm{Var}(\theta \mid m)\big].$$ Lower posterior variance means higher informational transmission and tighter anchoring of expectations to fundamentals.

The static model implies that higher $b$ mechanically reduces the feasible partition size $L$ via Proposition 1, which increases posterior variance. The repeated model implies that raising $\delta$ by making outcomes promptly observable can relax credibility constraints dramatically, potentially sustaining full revelation even when the static game would be highly coarse.

Corollary 1. Fix a target informativeness level interpreted as full revelation. Then the maximal bias that can coexist with that target in the repeated environment is $$b_{\max}(\delta) = \sqrt{\frac{\delta}{12(1 - \delta)}}.$$ In particular, $b_{\max}(\delta)$ is strictly increasing in $\delta$.

Proof. Immediate from Proposition 2, since the truth telling strategy is feasible precisely under that inequality, and the right hand side increases in $\delta$. $\square$

The corollary is a precise answer to the question of distance. To optimize the influence of transmission, understood as the ability to anchor expectations to the true state rather than to noise created by strategic misreporting, the central bank must either reduce its effective bias or increase the intertemporal value of credibility. Increasing the intertemporal value is operationally equivalent to shortening the verification lag, publishing evaluation criteria that make deviations detectable, and building institutions that survive long enough for reputational losses to matter.

6) Concluding synthesis

In static cheap talk, bias restricts the number of distinct actions the public can rationally take in response to the central bank's words. That restriction is not a matter of rhetoric, it is an equilibrium constraint that can be written as an inequality. In repeated environments with verifiable outcomes, persistent interaction transforms credibility into an asset, and the maximal sustainable bias becomes an explicit function of patience. The practical lesson is that credibility is neither a slogan nor an aesthetic preference. It is a state variable created by intertemporal incentives and supported by observability.
